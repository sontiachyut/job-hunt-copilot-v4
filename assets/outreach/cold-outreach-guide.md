# Cold Outreach Guidebook

## Status

Working draft. This document captures the cold-outreach strategy we are actively designing.

## Purpose

This guide is broader than an email template guide.

It should eventually cover:

- who to reach out to
- in what order to reach out
- what type of ask to make
- how to personalize
- how to write the email
- when and how to follow up
- how to track outreach systematically

## Current Recipient Groups

The user currently wants to target these groups:

1. ASU alumni connections
2. previous job connections
3. recruiting managers who post job openings on LinkedIn
4. people who may be working on that team, especially software engineers

## Recipient Priority

The current working priority order is:

1. recruiting managers who post the job on LinkedIn
2. team-adjacent people who may work in or near that area, especially software engineers
3. ASU alumni connections
4. previous job connections

This order can change later, but it is the current working strategy.

## Current Focus

For now, the guide is being designed first for these two profile types:

1. recruiting managers who post job openings on LinkedIn
2. people who may be working on that team, especially software engineers

Other profile types will be added later.

## Outreach Modes

### 1. One-Step Approach

This is the current primary approach.

Goal:
- use personalization, work overlap, and role fit in the same email
- move toward a low-friction CTA in that same email
- sound curious and thoughtful, not desperate for the job

Current working interpretation:

- the sender is not leading with "I want this job"
- the sender is leading with curiosity about the work
- the role is still mentioned clearly
- the role interest should feel like a natural result of the overlap, not a needy demand
- the CTA is a 15-minute Zoom to learn more about the work and team

### 2. Two-Step Approach

This is also desired, but the detailed flow is still open.

Goal:
- start from genuine curiosity about the recipient's work
- ask for a short learning conversation (for example, 15 minutes)
- do not begin with the direct role-oriented ask

## Follow-Up Cadence

- wait roughly one week between follow-ups
- maximum follow-up count: 3
- stop follow-up once the person replies

## Tracking

Cold outreach should be tracked systematically in a CRM-style way.

For now, the current database can be sufficient if it can track:

- company
- role
- contact
- recipient type
- outreach mode
- sent / replied / bounced / follow-up state
- last touch date
- next follow-up date
- notes

For now, this is sufficient. A separate CRM tool is not required yet if the current database can support the fields above cleanly.

## Mobile-First Constraint

Assume many recipients read email on their phone.

Therefore:

- avoid long dense paragraphs
- keep the message easy to scan
- keep structure clean
- make the key value visible quickly

## Drafting Principles for Current Focus Profiles

For recruiting managers and team-adjacent engineers, the email should generally do the following:

1. Start with the recipient's work
2. Find something genuinely interesting in that work
3. Add a small amount of specific appreciation when it feels earned
4. Ask a question or show curiosity about it
5. Make it clear that the sender wants to learn from them
6. Then explain who the sender is
7. Then explain what the sender is looking for
8. Then end with a low-friction CTA

The opening matters the most because the reader likely gives only a few seconds of attention before deciding whether to continue.

The overall posture should be:

- curious about the work
- respectful of the recipient's experience
- confident, not desperate
- interested in learning more because there is real overlap

The hook should feel stronger than a normal applicant message.

The current working formula is:

- their work
- one grounded note of appreciation
- real overlap
- curiosity to learn more

## Hook Source Priority

For the current approach, the hook should be built from the recipient's own LinkedIn profile first.

Priority order:

1. the recipient's present work
2. the recipient's past work
3. other distinctive profile signals from LinkedIn
4. only after that, broader team or role context if needed

Within those profile signals, prefer:

1. technical work
2. product or system work
3. problem-space ownership
4. only then generic managerial or recruiting context if nothing better exists

This outreach is person-to-person.

So the hook should usually be about:

- what the specific person has worked on
- what they seem to care about
- what overlap exists between their work and the sender's work or interests

It should not default to generic team-level language if the person's own profile gives enough material.

## Important Clarification for Manager Outreach

For this hook style, we do not want to praise someone merely for:

- hiring
- managing
- posting jobs

Those are weaker hooks.

For managers, the stronger hook is:

- what technical, product, or system work they have done
- what kinds of teams or problems they have worked closely with
- what overlap exists between that work and the sender's background

If the person is a pure recruiter without visible technical or problem-space signals, do not force artificial praise of recruiting activity. In that case, use a lighter role/work-context hook or treat that outreach style as weaker.

## Hook Construction Rule

When building the hook:

- first look for overlap between the recipient's work and the sender's work
- if overlap exists, use that overlap as the center of the hook
- if overlap does not exist, still open from the recipient's work and add one small grounded note of appreciation
- in both cases, the hook should lead into genuine curiosity to learn more

## Current Working Email Shape for These Profiles

### 1. Hook

Use the first 5 to 10 seconds to get attention.

The opening should:

- talk about the recipient's work
- optionally include one small grounded compliment or note of appreciation
- feel specific
- make the recipient want to keep reading

### 2. Who I Am

After the hook, the email should explain who the sender is.

This should stay compact.

It should not become a long biography.

### 3. What I Am Looking For

For the one-step mode, the sender should make it clear that:

- they saw the position
- they are interested in the role
- they want to work with that team / company
- they are reaching out because they believe there is real fit
- they are genuinely curious about the work the role and team are doing

This should not sound like begging for the job.

It should sound more like:

- "I saw the work"
- "I noticed overlap"
- "I want to learn more"
- "The role also feels like a strong fit"

For the current one-step mode, this role-interest part should be explicit enough that the recipient does not have to guess why the sender is reaching out.

### 4. CTA

Examples:

- "Do you have 15 minutes for a Zoom meeting?"

For now, the default CTA is a 15-minute Zoom conversation.

The CTA should stay simple and low-friction.

## What Else Likely Belongs in These Emails

In addition to the current structure above, the email will probably need:

1. A bridge sentence explaining why the sender chose this specific person
2. One compact proof point showing fit for the role or work area
3. A reason the recipient should care now
4. A clear but non-pushy transition from curiosity to role interest
5. One small grounded compliment or appreciative note in the hook when it strengthens the email

These pieces still need deeper design.

## Recommended Additions for These Two Profile Types

For recruiting managers and team-adjacent engineers, the email will likely work better if it also includes the following elements in compact form:

### 1. Why This Person

After the work hook, add one short bridge line explaining why this specific person felt like the right person to reach out to.

Examples of the intent:

- their work seems close to the problem area
- they appear to be working on the same kind of systems or product space
- their background suggests they have seen this work up close
- they posted the role and their own profile suggests connection to the work area

This helps the email feel intentional instead of random.

### 2. One Proof Point of Fit

Do not list too many credentials.

Use one strong proof point that makes the recipient believe the sender could genuinely fit the role or team.

This can be:

- one relevant project
- one relevant internship or job experience
- one relevant technical area
- one direct overlap with the role's work

### 3. Why Now

The reader should understand why the email is being sent now.

For these current profile types, the reason is usually:

- the sender saw the open role
- the sender is interested in that team or work area
- the sender believes there is real alignment
- the sender wants to learn more because the work genuinely overlaps with their own interests or experience

### 4. A Question That Feels Real

If the email opens from the recipient's work, the question should feel like a real curiosity, not a fake setup.

The question should be:

- small
- specific
- easy to understand
- closely tied to the work hook

### 4A. Grounded Appreciation

Light praise is allowed and can help strengthen the opening, but it should be used carefully.

Good appreciation:

- is tied to something specific
- feels earned
- sounds like a real reaction to the work

Bad appreciation:

- sounds generic
- sounds like flattery for its own sake
- could be pasted into any email

### 5. Phone-Friendly Layout

Even when the message has multiple ideas, it should still read well on a phone.

Practical writing constraints:

- short paragraphs
- avoid walls of text
- avoid stacking too many ideas in one paragraph
- the key point should appear early

### 6. One Clear Ask

Even if the email contains curiosity and fit, the final ask should stay singular.

For these two current profile types, the ask can usually be:

- a 15-minute Zoom conversation

For team-adjacent engineers, there may later be a forwarding/help angle too, but that should stay secondary unless it is clearly the better ask.

## Current Working One-Step Playbook for These Two Profiles

This is the current working structure, not the final polished playbook:

1. hook about the recipient's work
2. one short appreciative note if it feels earned
3. short bridge for why this specific person
4. who the sender is
5. what the sender is looking for
6. one proof point of fit
7. simple CTA

This structure is useful because it balances:

- attention
- relevance
- credibility
- action

## Working Posture for Role-Linked Cold Outreach

For job-linked cold outreach, the current desired posture is:

- not "please give me this job"
- not generic application follow-up
- not overly sales-like

Instead, the email should feel like:

- "I saw the kind of work this role and team are doing"
- "I genuinely liked or respected something specific about that work"
- "I noticed overlap with what I have done or want to grow in"
- "I am genuinely curious to learn more about that work"
- "I would value a short conversation because there seems to be real alignment"

The role should still be mentioned clearly.

But the emotional tone should be:

- interest in the work first
- fit second
- opportunity as a natural next step

### Clear working rule

For the current recruiter and team-member outreach, the email should sound like:

- "I noticed the work this role and team are doing."
- "I saw real overlap with my background and interests."
- "I want to learn more about that work from you."
- "If it makes sense, I would value a short 15-minute Zoom."

It should not sound like:

- "I am emailing because I badly want this job."
- "Please consider my application."
- "I am sending the same role-interest note to everyone."

The difference matters because the sender is trying to stand out from many other applicants who will email the same person with a direct ask.

## Desired Recipient Reaction

If this outreach is working well, the recipient should feel something close to:

1. this is not a mass email
2. this person actually looked me up
3. this person seems thoughtful
4. there is a real reason they contacted me
5. this is job-related, but it does not feel needy or desperate

In the best case, the recipient should feel:

- seen
- respected
- not manipulated
- clear about why the sender emailed
- open to replying to a short Zoom request

## Why This Structure Works

If the email starts directly with:

- "I am interested in this role"
- "Please consider me"

the recipient is more likely to classify it as just another applicant email.

If the email instead starts by:

- noticing something real about the recipient's work
- showing one small grounded note of appreciation
- showing overlap
- then clearly stating role interest

the frame changes to:

- this person may actually understand the work
- this person may be relevant
- this is not random outreach

That is the psychological shift we are trying to create.

## Balance Rule: Strong but Not Fake

The opening should be strong, but it should not feel manipulative.

The sequence should usually be:

1. small specific hook
2. light appreciation
3. overlap
4. role interest
5. 15-minute Zoom ask

Avoid:

- too much praise
- too long a curiosity section
- hiding the role interest until the very end
- making the recipient feel tricked into a job ask

The goal is:

- human
- thoughtful
- clear
- honest about why the sender is reaching out

## Feels Natural vs Feels Fake

This distinction matters a lot for cold outreach.

If the email feels fake, the recipient may immediately classify it as manipulative or mass-produced.

If it feels natural, the recipient is more likely to keep reading and remain open to replying.

### Feels Natural

#### 1. Specific observation

Mention something concrete from the recipient's work.

Example:

- "Your work on retrieval evaluation loops stood out to me."

Why it works:

- it sounds like the sender actually noticed something real

#### 2. Light appreciation

Appreciate one thing in a restrained way.

Example:

- "I liked that it seems focused on making these systems reliable in practice."

Why it works:

- it reacts to the work itself, not to the person's status or title

#### 3. Real overlap

Connect their work to the sender's work honestly.

Example:

- "That overlaps with the kind of backend AI tooling work I have been drawn to as well."

Why it works:

- it creates a believable bridge between recipient and sender

#### 4. Clear role interest

Say clearly that the sender saw the opening and is interested.

Example:

- "I also saw the Backend AI Infrastructure role and felt there was real alignment."

Why it works:

- it does not hide the real reason for outreach

#### 5. Small ask

Ask for something reasonable.

Example:

- "Would you be open to a 15-minute Zoom in the coming 2 weeks?"

Why it works:

- it respects the recipient's time

### Feels Fake

#### 1. Generic praise

Examples:

- "Your background is very impressive."
- "You have an amazing profile."
- "Your experience is truly inspiring."

Why it fails:

- it could be sent to almost anyone

#### 2. Praise with no substance

Example:

- "I love the work you're doing."

Why it fails:

- the sender does not actually say what work they mean

#### 3. Forced curiosity

Example:

- asking a question that clearly exists only to set up the job ask

Why it fails:

- the recipient can feel the hidden agenda too quickly

#### 4. Hidden agenda

Example:

- spending most of the email pretending it is only about learning
- then suddenly asking for job help or referral at the end

Why it fails:

- it makes the recipient feel tricked

#### 5. Too much admiration

Example:

- stacking multiple praise lines before saying anything concrete

Why it fails:

- it sounds manipulative
- it lowers credibility

#### 6. Too much self-selling too early

Example:

- mentioning one sentence about the recipient
- then dumping a large self-summary

Why it fails:

- the personalization starts to look decorative rather than real

## Quick Tests

Use these tests when reviewing a draft:

### Test 1

Could this line be sent to 50 other people with almost no change?

If yes, it is probably too generic and may feel fake.

### Test 2

Does the role interest appear honestly enough that the recipient will not feel tricked later?

If no, the email may feel manipulative.

## Safe Natural Formula

The safest working formula is:

1. one real thing about their work
2. one small appreciative note
3. one honest overlap
4. one clear role-interest line
5. one small ask

This usually feels human without becoming vague.

## Recipient-Type Playbooks (Current Working Draft)

These are not final templates.

They are working playbooks so the two profile types do not get treated as the same outreach problem.

### 1. Recruiting Manager Version

#### Core objective

Get the recruiting or hiring manager interested enough to give a short conversation or seriously consider the profile for the role.

This version can still start from the recipient's work, post, or hiring context.

The difference is that the work-centered opening acts as both:

- the attention hook
- the reason the sender is reaching out

The email should still pivot into role relevance and fit, but without losing the curiosity-led tone.

This means the recruiting-manager version is still allowed to say:

- "I want to learn more about the work behind this role and team"

The difference is just that it should connect that learning interest back to:

- the open position
- the sender's overlap
- the manager's decision context

#### What this recipient is likely to care about

- whether the sender fits the open role
- whether the sender understands what the role/team needs
- whether the email is relevant and worth reading now
- whether a short conversation would be a good use of time
- whether the sender sounds thoughtful rather than generic or desperate

#### Recommended flow

1. Start with something specific from their post or hiring context
2. Add a small grounded note of appreciation if it strengthens the hook
3. Optionally include a small question or note of curiosity if it helps the opening feel natural
4. Bridge into why they were the right person to contact
5. Introduce who the sender is in one compact line
6. State that the sender saw the role and feels genuine overlap with the work
7. Give one strong proof point of fit
8. Ask for a 15-minute Zoom

Practical interpretation:

- start from their work
- connect that work to the role
- connect the role to the sender's overlap
- ask for a short Zoom to learn more and explore fit

#### What belongs in the email

- a specific hook tied to their hiring post, team, or work area
- one small grounded compliment when it feels earned
- a short curiosity note when it strengthens the opening
- clear statement that the sender saw the role
- one compact reason the sender overlaps with the work and may be a strong fit
- a clean ask for a short Zoom conversation

#### What to avoid

- sounding like a generic application follow-up
- writing a long self-summary
- spending too much time on curiosity without connecting back to the role
- using too many proof points
- sounding needy or overly eager to "get the job"
- sounding flattering in a generic or manipulative way

#### Current working shape

```text
1. I noticed something specific in your hiring post / work.
2. A short appreciative note can appear here if it feels earned.
3. A short curiosity note or question can appear here if it feels natural.
4. That is why I wanted to reach out to you specifically.
5. Briefly, here is who I am.
6. I saw the role and felt real overlap with the work.
7. Here is one strong reason I may fit.
8. Would you be open to a 15-minute Zoom in the coming 2 weeks?
```

### 2. Team-Member / Software Engineer Version

#### Core objective

Build connection through their work, show genuine curiosity, and open the door to a short conversation that can later help with referral, forwarding, or internal visibility.

This version should feel more naturally work-centered than the recruiting-manager version.

The opening is not just a hook. It is often the real center of the email.

#### What this recipient is likely to care about

- whether the sender actually understands or appreciates their work
- whether the question feels genuine
- whether the sender seems thoughtful and relevant
- whether a short conversation would feel natural instead of transactional

#### Recommended flow

1. Start with a specific part of their work
2. Add a small grounded note of appreciation when it feels earned
3. Ask a real question or show genuine curiosity
4. Explain why this person felt worth reaching out to
5. Briefly introduce who the sender is
6. Mention the role interest and team fit
7. Give one compact proof point of relevant overlap
8. Ask for a 15-minute Zoom

Practical interpretation:

- stay in the work/curiosity zone a little longer
- let the conversation feel more peer-to-peer
- mention the role without making the email feel like a disguised application ask

#### What belongs in the email

- a work-centered hook
- one small grounded compliment when it feels natural
- a small, real question
- one line showing why the sender thought this person was the right person to learn from
- one strong overlap with the work area
- a simple ask

#### What to avoid

- sounding like the sender is using fake curiosity only to ask for a job
- jumping to referral/forwarding too early
- making the email too recruiter-like
- over-explaining the sender's background
- using hollow praise

#### Current working shape

```text
1. I saw something in your work that genuinely caught my attention.
2. I appreciated one specific part of it.
3. I had a quick question / wanted to learn how you think about it.
4. That is why I felt you were the right person to reach out to.
5. Briefly, here is who I am.
6. I also saw the role and felt there was real alignment.
7. Here is one short proof point of fit.
8. Would you be open to a 15-minute Zoom in the coming 2 weeks?
```

## Why These Two Versions Should Differ

Both versions can start from the recipient's work.

That part does not need to be different.

The real difference is what happens next.

The recruiting manager version should pivot faster into:

- the open role
- fit for that role
- why the sender is worth considering now

The team-member / software engineer version should stay longer in:

- curiosity
- work overlap
- peer-to-peer relevance

before moving into role interest.

In short:

- same human opening
- different destination

## Forwardable Snippet Guide

This section is based on what is already working in the current project:

- peer-level ICs are more likely to reply and forward
- making it easy to forward reduces friction
- copy-paste-friendly snippet blocks help
- the current composer already supports snippet-like copy-friendly formatting

### Purpose

The snippet exists to reduce effort for a helpful internal person.

It should make it easy for them to:

- forward the candidate internally
- paste a short note into IM or email
- recommend the candidate without rewriting everything

### When to use it

Use it mainly for:

- internal employees
- team-adjacent engineers
- alumni/helpful contacts
- people more likely to forward than directly decide

Do not make it standard for:

- direct hiring-manager outreach
- direct decision-maker outreach

unless there is a clear reason.

### Format

The snippet should be:

- very short
- easy to copy
- easy to forward
- easy to understand in one glance

Current working rule:

- roughly 3 lines
- feels like something the current recipient could plausibly send
- not a mini-resume pasted into the email

### Content

The snippet should usually contain:

1. who the candidate is
2. what role or work area this is about
3. 1 to 2 strongest reasons they may fit

### Tone

The snippet should feel like:

- a helpful internal note
- written on behalf of the forwarder
- concise and low-effort

It should not feel like:

- marketing copy
- something awkward to forward
- an over-packed candidate summary

## Subject Line Guide

Subject lines matter because they are often the first filter in a crowded inbox.

The subject should be:

- short
- specific
- easy to scan on mobile
- relevant to the recipient type
- aligned with either the role or the work hook

### Strong subject-line directions

Good subject lines usually do one of these:

1. mention the role clearly
2. mention the work area clearly
3. combine company + work area naturally

### What to avoid

Avoid subject lines that are:

- vague
- salesy
- clickbait-like
- overly long
- too clever

Examples to avoid:

- `Quick question`
- `Opportunity`
- `Interested candidate`
- `Can you help me?`

### Current direction by recipient type

For hiring-manager / manager outreach:

- role-aware subject lines are usually stronger

For engineer / team-member outreach:

- work-aware subject lines are usually stronger

### Current example directions

- `Backend AI infra role at HelioStack`
- `Your retrieval systems work at HelioStack`

## Research Checklist Before Writing

Before drafting, gather the following from LinkedIn:

1. present role
2. past roles
3. technical / product / system work
4. problem areas they seem close to
5. posts, projects, or focus areas
6. strongest overlap with the sender
7. whether they seem like a decision-maker, helper, or forwarder

Then gather the following from the JD:

1. exact role title
2. core work area
3. technical themes / stack
4. what kind of engineer the team seems to need
5. strongest overlap between JD and sender background

Then gather from the tailored resume / sender profile:

1. one strongest overlap point
2. one strongest credibility point
3. one clear reason this role makes sense now

### Final pre-draft selection

Before writing, explicitly choose:

1. one hook
2. one appreciative note if needed
3. one overlap point
4. one role-interest line
5. one CTA

If the hook is weak, do not force it.

## Review Checklist Before Sending

Use this checklist when reviewing a draft:

### Hook quality

1. Is the opening tied to the recipient's real work?
2. Is the appreciation light and specific?
3. Does it feel person-to-person rather than generic?

### Honesty and clarity

4. Is the role interest explicit?
5. Is the real reason for outreach clear?
6. Does the draft avoid sounding desperate?

### Fit and credibility

7. Is there one believable overlap point?
8. Is the self-description compact and relevant?

### Readability

9. Is it short enough for phone reading?
10. Are the paragraphs easy to scan?

### Ask quality

11. Is there only one ask?
12. Is the ask a simple 15-minute Zoom?

### Optional snippet check

13. If a forwardable snippet is included, is the recipient actually the kind of person who may forward it?
14. Is the snippet short and low-friction?

## Worked Example Drafts (Made-Up Scenario)

These examples use a fictional company, role, and contacts.

These are approved baseline examples for the current outreach style.

They are not universal templates, but they are strong reference drafts for the current one-step approach.

Their purpose is to test tone, structure, and the difference between recipient types.

### Scenario

- Company: `HelioStack`
- Role: `Backend Software Engineer, AI Infrastructure`
- Candidate overlap:
  - built retrieval-backed internal AI tools
  - worked on API/backend systems
  - interested in production AI infrastructure and developer tooling

### 1. Hiring Manager Example (V1 Baseline)

#### Context

- Recipient: `Maya Chen`
- Role: Engineering Manager at HelioStack
- Signal: LinkedIn profile mentions backend platform work, distributed systems experience, and current ownership of AI infrastructure teams

#### Subject

Backend AI infra role at HelioStack

#### Draft

```text
Hi Maya,

I came across your profile while looking into HelioStack, and your background in backend platform and distributed systems work stood out to me right away. I liked that because the AI infrastructure role seems grounded in real systems work, which is exactly the space I have been trying to grow in.

I saw the Backend Software Engineer, AI Infrastructure opening and it felt closely aligned with the kind of work I want to keep building. I am a software engineer with experience in backend APIs and retrieval-backed AI tooling, and I have also worked on internal tools where reliability and practical AI use mattered more than demo quality, so there felt like real overlap.

I would love to learn a little more about how your team thinks about that work inside HelioStack and whether my background could be relevant for the role. Would you be open to a 15-minute Zoom in the coming 2 weeks?

Best,
Achyut
```

#### Why this works

- opens from the recipient's own LinkedIn profile context
- uses technical background rather than praising hiring activity
- stays person-to-person rather than team-to-person
- makes the role interest explicit without sounding needy
- keeps the curiosity tied to the actual work area
- asks for a simple 15-minute Zoom

Your retrieval systems work at HelioStack

#### Why this works
